## üåç Scope 3: Value Chain Indirect Emissions (15 Categories) - **Impacts & Opportunities**

Scope 3 covers **all other indirect emissions** that occur across the company's value chain, from suppliers to product disposal. It often accounts for **70-90%** of a company's total footprint, making its proactive management a strategic necessity.

### Upstream Activities (Categories 1-8)
These emissions are related to activities and products purchased by the company.

| Category | Description | Key Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Purchased Goods and Services** | Emissions from the **production** of all purchased materials, components, and services. | Raw materials, packaging, IT services, consulting fees. |
| **2. Capital Goods** | Emissions from the **production** of long-lived assets used to produce other goods or services. | Machinery, equipment, buildings, vehicles purchased for the company fleet. |
| **3. Fuel- and Energy-Related Activities (Not in Scope 1 or 2)** | Emissions related to the **extraction, processing, and distribution** of fuels and energy purchased by the company. | Transmission and distribution (T\&D) losses for purchased electricity. |
| **4. Upstream Transportation and Distribution** | Emissions from third-party transport and storage of **purchased products** between the supplier and the company's facility. | Shipping raw materials from a supplier to the company's facility. |
| **5. Waste Generated in Operations** | Emissions from the **disposal and treatment** of waste and wastewater generated in the company's own facilities. | Methane release from office trash sent to a landfill. |
| **6. Business Travel** | Emissions from employees traveling for business purposes in vehicles **not owned or controlled** by the company. | Commercial flights, rail travel, hotel stays. |
| **7. Employee Commuting** | Emissions from the daily travel of employees between their homes and their worksites. | Employee driving personal cars, using public transit. |
| **8. Upstream Leased Assets** | Emissions from the operation of assets leased **by** the reporting company. | Rented office space, leased equipment. |

### Downstream Activities (Categories 9-15)
These emissions are related to the company's sold products and services, occurring after the product has left the company's control.

| Category | Description | Key Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **9. Downstream Transportation and Distribution** | Emissions from third-party transport and storage of **sold products** between the company's facility and the end consumer or retailer. | Shipping finished goods from the company warehouse to the customer. |
| **10. Processing of Sold Products** | Emissions from the further processing of **intermediate products** sold by the company to a third party. | A textile manufacturer selling fabric that a customer then uses to make clothing. |
| **11. Use of Sold Products** | Emissions from the end-user's use of the company's final products. | Energy consumption by an appliance or a car sold by the company. |
| **12. End-of-Life Treatment of Sold Products** | Emissions from the disposal or recycling of the company's products after the customer is finished with them. | Emissions from the decomposition or incineration of discarded packaging or products. |
| **13. Downstream Leased Assets** | Emissions from assets **owned** by the reporting company but leased **to** other entities. | Commercial properties or equipment leased to third parties. |
| **14. Franchises** | Emissions from the operation of facilities or businesses that operate under the company's franchise license. | Emissions from the daily operations of a franchised restaurant location. |
| **15. Investments** | Emissions associated with investments made by the reporting company (relevant to financial institutions). | Emissions from companies in which a financial institution has invested. |

## üìà Proactive Scope 3 Management: Impacts and Opportunities for SMEs

Managing Scope 3 proactively transforms it from a data burden into a strategic asset for SMEs.

### Commercial & Market Opportunities

| Opportunity | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Access to Major Contracts (CSRD)** | Large companies (subject to regulations like CSRD) are desperately seeking suppliers (SMEs) that can provide credible, verifiable Scope 3 data to meet their own reporting needs. **Proactive SMEs become "preferred suppliers."** |
| **Attract Capital & Financing** | Banks and investors are increasingly using ESG performance as a core risk and valuation metric. Verified Scope 3 data unlocks access to **green loans, sustainable financing**, and attracts ESG-focused venture capital/investors. |
| **Product Innovation & Pricing** | Identifying high-emission suppliers or materials (Cat 1) allows the SME to redesign products with lower-carbon alternatives, often creating **cost efficiencies** or justifying a **premium price** for a "sustainable" product line. |
| **Competitive Differentiation** | Being an early, credible reporter in an industry (especially relative to peers) builds brand reputation and **attracts environmentally conscious customers** and top talent. |

### Operational Impacts

| Impact Area | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Risk Mitigation (Supply Chain)** | Identifying emission "hotspots" in the supply chain (Cat 1, 4) often correlates with **resource dependency, price volatility (fuel/materials)**, and vulnerability to climate-related disruptions. Proactive management builds resilience. |
| **Cost Savings (Efficiency)** | Analyzing categories like Business Travel (Cat 6) and Waste (Cat 5) frequently uncovers operational inefficiencies that lead to **immediate cost reductions** (e.g., optimizing logistics routes, minimizing waste disposal fees). |
| **Data Quality Improvement** | The process forces the SME to gain a **deeper, clearer understanding of its own value chain**‚Äîwho its key suppliers are, what materials are truly used, and where the most significant spend lies. |
